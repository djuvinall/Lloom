The water cycle describes how water moves through the environment. Water evaporates from oceans and lakes, rises as vapor, and cools to form clouds. When the droplets grow heavy enough, they fall back to the surface as rain or snow.

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants turn sunlight into food. Using energy from the sun, a plant combines water and carbon dioxide to make sugar and release oxygen. Almost every food chain on land begins with this quiet chemistry.

Gravity is the force that pulls objects toward one another. The more massive an object, the stronger its pull. Earth's gravity keeps the moon in orbit and gives every falling apple its hurry.

A library is a collection of books and other materials organized for reading and study. Public libraries lend their holdings freely, which makes knowledge available to anyone who walks through the door.

Bees live together in colonies and divide the work among themselves. Foragers gather nectar and pollen, nurses tend the young, and the colony shares whatever each member brings home.

The printing press changed the world by making books cheap to copy. Before it, every volume was written out by hand, so few people owned even one. After it, ideas could travel farther and faster than ever before.

Mountains form slowly as the great plates of the Earth's crust push against each other. Rock that once lay flat is folded and lifted over millions of years. What looks permanent to us is, on a longer clock, still moving.

A good night of sleep helps the body repair itself and the mind sort through the day. People who sleep well tend to remember more and feel steadier. Rest is not wasted time; it is part of how we work.

Rivers carry water from high ground down to the sea. Along the way they shape valleys, feed farmland, and carry boats and goods. A single river can connect towns that would otherwise be strangers.

Curiosity is the engine of learning. A question you actually care about will pull you through difficult material that no schedule could force you to finish. The trick is to keep asking better questions.

Maps are small drawings that stand in for large places. A useful map leaves out almost everything and keeps only what the traveler needs. Knowing what to ignore is half of making one.

Salt has been valuable for most of human history because it preserves food. Long before refrigerators, a layer of salt let meat and fish last through the winter. Whole trade routes grew up around it.

The seasons change because the Earth is tilted as it circles the sun. For part of the year your half of the planet leans toward the light, and for part of it leans away. The tilt, not the distance, makes summer and winter.

Practice works best in small, regular doses. A little effort every day adds up faster than a single long burst once a week, because the brain consolidates skills during the gaps. Consistency beats intensity.

Wind is simply air on the move, pushed from places of high pressure toward places of low pressure. The sun heats the ground unevenly, the air above it shifts, and we feel the result as a breeze or a gale.

A compass points north because the Earth itself behaves like a giant magnet. The needle, free to swing, lines up with that invisible field. With it, a traveler can hold a direction even when the sky is hidden.
